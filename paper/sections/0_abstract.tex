\begin{abstract}
In open-world rule reasoning, an \texttt{Unknown} answer is only useful if the
system can say what support is missing. We study this requirement in
ProofWriter, where a query may be entailed, contradicted, or unsupported by an
explicit natural-language theory. We introduce \model, a supervision format that
uses one schema for proving true queries, refuting false queries, and abstaining
with a missing-support witness. A lightweight symbolic verifier checks the
emitted evidence against the theory closure. On a three-seed 7B study,
\model{} raises joint label-plus-evidence accuracy from 39.9\% to 75.0\%
in-domain and from 26.5\% to 55.0\% under depth shift relative to proof-only
supervision, while raw label accuracy remains comparable. Proof-only often
predicts \texttt{Unknown}, but its abstentions are unverifiable; \model{} makes
many of them checkable. A support-deletion stress test, built by removing
facts or rules from originally provable questions, shows the same pattern.
Missing-support supervision therefore turns abstention from a bare label into
checkable evidence in this controlled rule-reasoning setting.
\end{abstract}
