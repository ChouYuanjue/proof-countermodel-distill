\begin{abstract}
In open-world rule reasoning, answering \texttt{Unknown} is not enough: a system
should also say which support is missing. We study this problem on ProofWriter,
where a query can be entailed, contradicted, or unsupported by the same
natural-language theory. We introduce \model, a supervised output format that
asks the model to either prove the query, refute it by deriving the opposite
literal, or abstain with a failed-rule or missing-support witness. A lightweight
symbolic verifier then checks the generated evidence against the theory closure.
The main question is concrete: when models abstain, are their abstentions
checkable? On a fixed three-seed 7B suite with 4,096 training questions,
\model{} more than doubles joint label-plus-evidence accuracy over proof-only
supervision, from 39.9\% to 75.0\% in-domain and from 26.5\% to 55.0\% on a
depth-5 test subset. This gain is not caused by abstaining more often. On
gold-unknown examples, proof-only and \model{} predict \texttt{Unknown} at
similar rates, but proof-only produces zero faithful unknown explanations while
\model{} produces 1,444 in-domain and 1,169 on depth-5. Raw accuracy remains
competitive rather than dominant at small training budgets, and improves with
scale: with full training, \model{} reaches 98.2\% in-domain accuracy and
96.8\% joint accuracy, and reaches 93.8\% accuracy and 86.2\% joint accuracy on
depth-5. These results support a focused claim: missing-support witness
supervision turns abstention from a label into verifiable evidence for
ProofWriter-style logical reasoning.
\end{abstract}
