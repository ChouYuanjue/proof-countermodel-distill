\begin{abstract}
In open-world rule reasoning, an \texttt{Unknown} answer is only useful if the
system can say what support is missing. We study this requirement in
ProofWriter-style indexed rule reasoning, where a query may be entailed,
contradicted, or unsupported by an explicit theory. We introduce \model, a
supervision format that uses one schema for proving true queries, refuting false
queries, and abstaining with a missing-support witness. A lightweight symbolic
verifier checks emitted evidence against the theory closure. On a three-seed
7B study, \model{} raises joint label-plus-verifier-accepted-evidence accuracy
from 39.9\% to 75.0\% in-domain and from 26.5\% to 55.0\% under depth shift
relative to proof-only supervision, while raw label accuracy remains
competitive with proof-only. At the 4k budget, answer-only remains the stronger
raw classifier, so the value of \model{} is checkable evidence rather than raw
classification superiority. Proof-only often predicts \texttt{Unknown}, but it
is not trained to emit missing-support witnesses; \model{} makes many of those
abstentions checkable. A support-deletion stress test, built by removing facts
or rules from originally provable questions, shows the same pattern under a
controlled intervention. Missing-support supervision therefore turns abstention
from a bare label into checkable evidence in this controlled setting.
\end{abstract}
