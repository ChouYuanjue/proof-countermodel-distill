\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Neural proof generation and benchmark design.}
ProofWriter \citep{tafjord2020proofwriter} evaluates natural-language rule
reasoning with facts, rules, queries, labels, and proof structure. It is useful
for our question because the same theory can contain entailed, contradicted, and
unsupported queries. More recent benchmark work has expanded prover-backed
logical-reasoning evaluation, including ProverGen/ProverQA
\citep{qi2025provergen}. Our work uses this benchmark family as a controlled
setting for a specific output requirement: unsupported queries should come with
checkable missing-support witnesses.

\paragraph{Solver-coupled logical reasoning.}
Logic-LM \citep{pan2023logiclm} couples a language model to a symbolic solver.
This gives a hard correctness constraint once the translation into symbolic form
is correct. \model{} asks a different question: if the test-time output is still
model-generated text, can supervision make the evidence easier to verify? Our
verifier checks the emitted evidence after generation; it does not search for a
better proof or abstention witness.

\paragraph{Symbolic chain-of-thought and reasoning verification.}
SymbCoT \citep{xu2024symbcot} uses symbolic chain-of-thought for logical
reasoning, and VeriCoT \citep{feng2025vericot} validates generated chains with
logical consistency checks. These methods motivate our use of a checker. The
difference is the object being checked: we check not only successful proof
chains, but also refutation chains and failed-rule or missing-support witnesses.

\paragraph{Concurrent formal disproof generation.}
Recent concurrent work by \citet{li2026learningtodisprove} studies formal
counterexample generation in Lean. Their framework synthesizes false formal
statements by dropping theorem hypotheses, trains with multi-reward expert
iteration, and evaluates machine-checked counterexamples. This is the closest
recent work in motivation, but not in task. They focus on false formal
statements; we focus on natural-language rule theories with true, false, and
unsupported queries. Their central output is a counterexample. Our central
output is a missing-support witness for \texttt{Unknown}.

\paragraph{Abstention and selective answering.}
Recent work studies whether large language models know when not to answer
\citep{madhusudhan2025abstention}. Standard abstention work often evaluates the
decision to refuse, defer, or say unknown. In our setting, the abstention label
is not enough. Because the theory is explicit, the abstention can be checked:
the model should identify missing support that is actually absent.

\paragraph{Position of this paper.}
This paper sits between proof generation, reasoning verification, and
abstention. The method claim is narrow: we change what the model is trained to
emit, and then measure whether those outputs are checkable. The distinctive
case is not proof or formal disproof, but abstention with evidence.
