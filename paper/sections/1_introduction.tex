\section{Introduction}

Consider a natural-language rule theory with facts, rules, and a query. If the
query follows from the theory, a model can justify \texttt{True} by giving a
proof chain. If the opposite literal follows, it can justify \texttt{False} by
giving a refutation chain. The difficult third case is \texttt{Unknown}: neither
the query nor its opposite follows. A model can output the right label while
still giving no checkable reason why the theory is insufficient.

This paper studies that third case. In ProofWriter \citep{tafjord2020proofwriter},
the same theory can generate true, false, and unknown queries under open-world
semantics. Existing logical-reasoning pipelines have emphasized proof
generation, solver-coupled inference, symbolic chain-of-thought, and validation
of generated reasoning \citep{pan2023logiclm,xu2024symbcot,feng2025vericot,qi2025provergen}.
Work on abstention asks when models should decline to answer
\citep{madhusudhan2025abstention}. We ask a stricter question for rule
reasoning: when the gold answer is \texttt{Unknown}, can the model produce a
missing-support witness that a symbolic checker can verify?

The distinction between \texttt{False} and \texttt{Unknown} is important. Recent
concurrent work by \citet{li2026learningtodisprove} studies formal
counterexample generation in Lean: given false formal statements, the goal is to
produce machine-checked counterexamples. Our setting is different. We do not
generate Lean counterexamples or synthesize mutated formal theorems. We study
natural-language rule theories where unsupported queries are neither true nor
false, and where the missing object is an explanation of absent support.

We use a minimal intervention. Instead of adding a search controller or solver
at test time, we change the training target. \model{} uses one output schema with
three modes:
\begin{itemize}
\item \prove{}: derive the queried literal with a symbolic chain.
\item \refute{}: derive the opposite literal as a falsity witness.
\item \abstain{}: expose the missing support or failed rule that blocks derivation.
\end{itemize}
For \abstain{}, the witness can be checked against the theory closure: the
claimed missing support must actually be absent. Evaluation therefore reports
both raw label accuracy and joint label-plus-evidence accuracy.

The main result is not raw-accuracy domination. At 7B with 4,096 training
questions and a fixed three-seed test subset, answer-only remains the strongest
raw classifier. But among systems that emit evidence, \model{} gives a large
gain in verifiable reasoning: joint accuracy rises from 39.9 to 75.0 in-domain
and from 26.5 to 55.0 on depth-5 compared with proof-only supervision. As the
training budget increases, the raw-accuracy gap contracts. With the full
training set, \model{} reaches 98.2\% in-domain accuracy with 96.8\% joint
accuracy, and 93.8\% depth-5 accuracy with 86.2\% joint accuracy on the fixed
subset.

The clearest mechanism appears on gold-unknown examples. Proof-only supervision
already learns roughly when to abstain: it predicts \texttt{Unknown} 1,544 times
in-domain and 1,278 times on depth-5, close to \model{}'s 1,525 and 1,286.
However, those proof-only abstentions are unverifiable. They yield zero faithful
unknown explanations. \model{} yields 1,444 faithful unknown explanations
in-domain and 1,169 on depth-5. The improvement is therefore not that the model
abstains more often; it is that abstentions become checkable.

The paper's claim is deliberately narrow. We do not present a theorem-prover
system, a formal disproof engine, or a general solution to logical reasoning.
The contribution is a focused supervised study of missing-support witnesses for
natural-language rule reasoning.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We define missing-support witnesses for unsupported ProofWriter-style
queries and train models to emit them with an explicit \abstain{} mode.
\item We implement a symbolic checker that verifies proof chains, refutation
chains, and abstention witnesses against the theory closure.
\item We provide seed-controlled, scaling, transfer, and ablation experiments
showing that witness supervision improves checkable abstention and joint
label-plus-evidence correctness, while raw accuracy becomes competitive at
larger training budgets.
\end{enumerate}
