\section{Introduction}

Logical reasoning systems are often evaluated by whether they answer correctly.
For open-world rule reasoning, this is incomplete. If a theory does not entail a
query or its negation, the correct label is \texttt{Unknown}; but a bare
\texttt{Unknown} does not explain why the theory is insufficient. A useful
system should identify the missing support that blocks the derivation.

ProofWriter \citep{tafjord2020proofwriter} makes this question concrete. Each
example contains natural-language facts and rules, and each query is
\texttt{True}, \texttt{False}, or \texttt{Unknown} under open-world semantics.
Proof and refutation have natural evidence: derive the query, or derive the
opposite literal. The unsupported case needs a different kind of evidence: a
witness that some needed premise or rule application is absent.

We study whether this behavior can be learned through supervision alone.
\model{} uses a single structured output format with three modes:
\prove{} emits a derivation chain for true queries, \refute{} emits a derivation
of the opposite literal for false queries, and \abstain{} emits a failed-rule or
missing-support witness for unsupported queries. A symbolic checker then
replays proof and refutation chains and checks abstention witnesses against the
theory closure. The central metric is joint accuracy: the label must be correct
and the evidence must verify.

The main empirical result is simple. On a fixed three-seed 7B suite,
\model{} keeps raw accuracy close to proof-only supervision but more than
doubles joint accuracy: 39.9\% to 75.0\% in-domain and 26.5\% to 55.0\% under
depth shift. The mechanism is also clear. Proof-only models often learn when to
say \texttt{Unknown}, but their abstentions are generic and receive no verifier
credit; \model{} turns many of those abstentions into checkable witnesses. A
support-deletion stress test, where facts or rules are removed from originally
provable questions until they become unsupported, shows the same behavior under
a controlled intervention.

The study is scoped to supervised model outputs rather than solver-coupled
inference. The question is whether changing the target alone can make
model-generated reasoning easier to verify. This scope leaves important
limitations: the experiments rely on ProofWriter's indexed facts and rules, and
a free-text setting would need an additional alignment layer before the same
checks could apply.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We define missing-support witnesses for unsupported ProofWriter-style
queries and train models to emit them alongside proof and refutation evidence.
\item We implement a lightweight verifier for proof chains, refutation chains,
and abstention witnesses.
\item We introduce a support-deletion stress test for unsupported cases created
from originally provable questions.
\item We provide seeded, scaling, transfer, and ablation results showing that
missing-support supervision improves checkable abstention while keeping raw
accuracy competitive.
\end{enumerate}
