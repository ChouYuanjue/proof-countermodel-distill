\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\model{} improves the part of open-world reasoning that ordinary proof
supervision leaves underspecified: explaining why an unsupported query cannot
be derived. Proof-only models often learn the \texttt{Unknown} label, but their
reasons are generic or unverifiable. Missing-support supervision gives the
model a concrete object to emit and gives the verifier a concrete object to
check.

The result should be read with its scope in mind. The experiments use
ProofWriter-style indexed facts and rules, so the checker can align generated
evidence to symbolic objects. That is enough to test whether supervision alone
can make abstention auditable under a fixed generation loop, but it is not a
claim about general free-text logical reasoning or solver-free inference in the
wild.

We presented \model, a supervision format for proof, refutation, and
missing-support abstention. On ProofWriter-style indexed rule reasoning, it
turns many otherwise generic \texttt{Unknown} answers into verifier-accepted
evidence while preserving competitive raw accuracy at the 4k budget. The main
value is checkable abstention evidence, not a wholesale replacement for
solver-coupled inference.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

This work is limited to ProofWriter-style indexed facts and rules. Extending it
to free-text settings would require an NL-to-symbol alignment layer for
entities, literals, and rule paraphrases before the same checks could apply.
The target constructor and verifier also share closure and blocked-derivation
logic, so evaluation is coupled to the symbolic target-generation procedure.
The deterministic canonicalization used for training is therefore only a
heuristic; it is not a proof of global minimality, uniqueness, or causal
necessity.

The tolerant verifier is intentionally permissive: it accepts exact tag matches,
exact witness matches, or witnesses that mention all canonical missing leaves.
That makes the metric robust to surface variation, but it can over-credit
witnesses with extra or irrelevant content. The low strict
\texttt{FAIL[ruleX]} match rate shows that multiple grounded blocked derivations
can plausibly explain the same unsupported query, and an equivalence-aware
verifier remains future work. We also do not claim a human-utility result:
verifier-accepted evidence is compatible with the symbolic theory, but we do not
evaluate whether people find the witness understandable, sufficient, or useful
for debugging.

The support-deletion benchmark is a clean single-deletion stress test, not a
noisy real-world missing-evidence benchmark. It does not cover multi-support
deletion, paraphrase, entity aliasing, or noisy rules. Some backbone, scaling,
and ablation checks are single-seed, so they localize behavior rather than fully
characterize variance. The main neural comparison also does not include a
solver-coupled inference system; instead, the appendix reports a post-hoc
symbolic repair baseline to contextualize that alternative. Finally, the
current scope does not establish general free-text logical reasoning.
