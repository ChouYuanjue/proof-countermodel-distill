\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Full Study Supports}

The completed study supports a focused claim about checkable abstention. At
0.5B, \model{} mainly improves evidence quality. At 7B, the result becomes more
useful: on the fixed 4k seeded suite, \model{} more than doubles proof-only joint
accuracy in-domain and on depth-OOD, while raw accuracy remains close to the
structured baseline. As the training budget grows, the raw-accuracy gap to
answer-only narrows sharply. The tradeoff is therefore scale-sensitive rather
than fixed. The support-deletion mutation result adds a controlled
intervention: when a necessary fact or rule is removed from an otherwise
provable question, proof-only often predicts \texttt{Unknown} but still cannot
explain the missing support, while \model{} frequently can. The abstention gain
therefore survives beyond naturally occurring unknown questions.

\subsection{Why \model{} Helps}

The main effect is abstention quality. Proof-only and \model{} predict
\texttt{Unknown} on similar numbers of gold-unknown examples, but proof-only
does not produce faithful missing-support explanations. \model{} does. The
abstention ablations show that both parts of the target are useful: failed-rule
tags and witness text each recover much of the gain, and the full target is best
or tied. The no-refute ablation gives a useful boundary. Removing explicit
refutation leaves much of the unknown-case behavior intact, but lowers overall
joint accuracy because false predictions no longer have checkable refutation
chains. The distinctive contribution is unsupported-query supervision, while
refutation supervision remains necessary for full label-plus-evidence coverage.
The support-deletion split gives a second boundary: \model{} handles deleted
rules better than deleted facts, suggesting that some surface fact omissions
remain hard even when the model has learned the abstention format.

\subsection{Relation to Concurrent Formal Disproof Work}

\citet{li2026learningtodisprove} study formal counterexample generation in Lean
using symbolic mutation and multi-reward expert iteration. Their work makes it
important to be precise about our scope. We do not claim to introduce disproof as
a task, and we do not compete with Lean counterexample generation. Our problem is
unsupported natural-language rule queries: cases where neither the query nor its
opposite follows, and where the desired evidence is missing support rather than a
counterexample.

\subsection{Scaling Behavior}

Scaling changes how the result should be read. At low budget, witness
supervision buys much better evidence at some raw-accuracy cost. At larger
budgets, that cost shrinks. On the full-train fixed depth-5 subset, \model{}
slightly surpasses answer-only in raw accuracy, and on transfer settings it
remains competitive while preserving large joint-accuracy gains. The practical
lesson is that missing-support supervision becomes more attractive as the model
and training budget improve.

\subsection{Limitations}

This is not a solved logical-reasoning result. First, the study uses one
benchmark family, so the strongest claims are limited to ProofWriter-style rule
reasoning. Second, answer-only systems receive joint accuracy 0 by construction
because they do not emit evidence; joint accuracy must therefore be read together
with raw accuracy. Third, Mistral and several ablations are single-seed
measurements. They localize the effect, but do not fully characterize variance.

\subsection{Next Steps}

The natural next step is to stress-test the same supervision idea beyond the current setting. The most useful directions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{backbone breadth}: repeat the Mistral main and support-deletion
experiments with multiple seeds,
\item \textbf{harder mutation}: delete combinations of supports or construct
near-miss cases where one branch remains derivable but another is blocked,
\item \textbf{benchmark breadth}: evaluate on additional verifier-backed
reasoning datasets with unsupported cases,
\item \textbf{answer/rationale decoupling}: combine a short answer channel with a
separate verifier-constrained evidence channel.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Conclusion}

We presented \model, a supervision format that asks models to prove, refute, or
abstain with a missing-support witness. The main result is concrete:
proof-oriented supervision can learn to say \texttt{Unknown}, but it does not
make those abstentions checkable. Missing-support witness supervision closes
that gap on ProofWriter-style reasoning, producing large gains in joint
label-plus-evidence accuracy while raw accuracy becomes competitive with more
training data.
