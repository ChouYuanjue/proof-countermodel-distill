\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

We use the processed OWA version of ProofWriter. After flattening each theory into question-level instances, the \texttt{depth-3ext-NatLang} split contains 112,062 training questions, 17,312 development questions, and 34,378 test questions. The \texttt{depth-5} split contains 36,902 training questions, 10,190 development questions, and 20,030 test questions. We also use \texttt{NatLang/test}, \texttt{depth-3/test}, and \texttt{birds-electricity/test} for transfer evaluation.

Our study has two layers. First, we run a \textbf{seeded 7B stability suite} on a fixed 4,096-question training budget and a fixed sampled test subset. The training seed changes the sampled training questions, but the evaluation subset is held constant with a fixed evaluation data seed so that seed variance reflects model behavior rather than a moving test sample. Second, we scale the same comparison to larger training budgets and evaluate selected checkpoints on full test splits for transfer analysis.

We compare three canonical supervision formats:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{answer-only}: predict only the label,
\item \textbf{proof-only}: emit structured reasoning without countermodel targets,
\item \textbf{\model{}}: emit proof, refutation, or abstention witnesses.
\end{itemize}
We also study two abstention-target ablations:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ProCo-chain}: keep abstention failure tags but replace detailed witnesses with a generic note,
\item \textbf{ProCo-witness}: keep detailed missing-support witnesses but remove explicit failure tags.
\end{itemize}

All runs use QLoRA with NF4 4-bit loading, LoRA rank 16, LoRA alpha 32, dropout 0.05, maximum sequence length 512, learning rate \(2\times 10^{-4}\), and one training epoch. We evaluate label accuracy, macro-F1, Unknown-class F1, evidence faithfulness, and joint accuracy. Faithfulness checks whether the emitted chain or abstention witness is consistent with the theory; joint accuracy requires both a correct label and faithful evidence.

\subsection{7B Seeded Results on a Fixed Test Subset}

The main 7B question is whether the 4k story survives seed variation once the comparison is made on a fixed sampled test subset. Table~\ref{tab:seeded-results} reports the resulting mean and standard deviation across three seeds. This table is the core robustness result because it converts earlier single-seed observations into a stable estimate of the accuracy-faithfulness tradeoff.

\begin{table}[t]

\label{tab:seeded-results}
\end{table}

The seeded result is clear. Answer-only remains the strongest raw classifier at this small training budget, but \model{} delivers a large and highly stable gain in verifier-backed reasoning. In-domain, \model{} reaches \(75.0 \pm 0.7\) joint accuracy versus \(39.9 \pm 0.6\) for proof-only, while raw accuracy changes only from \(87.9 \pm 0.2\) to \(87.4 \pm 0.3\). On depth-OOD, the same pattern holds: \(55.0 \pm 0.7\) joint accuracy for \model{} versus \(26.5 \pm 1.1\) for proof-only, with nearly identical raw accuracy (\(76.4 \pm 0.6\) versus \(76.1 \pm 0.7\)). Relative to answer-only, the raw-accuracy gap is 1.5 points in-domain and 3.6 points on depth-OOD, but answer-only provides no verifier-backed evidence at all. The right interpretation is therefore not ``\model{} wins every metric,'' but that it robustly converts a small raw-accuracy concession into a large gain in faithful reasoning.

\subsection{Where the Gain Comes From: Unknown-Case Behavior}

The most important behavioral difference is not simply that \model{} predicts \texttt{Unknown} more often. Table~\ref{tab:unknown-behavior-maintrack} focuses on gold-unknown examples and asks a sharper question: when the model abstains, does it abstain \emph{faithfully}? This is where proof-only supervision and \model{} differ most clearly.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\input{generated/unknown_table.tex}
\caption{Unknown-case behavior on the fixed 7B test subset. The main mechanism claim is that \model{} improves faithful abstention witnesses rather than merely changing the abstention rate.}
\label{tab:unknown-behavior-maintrack}
\end{table}

This table operationalizes the mechanism claim directly. On gold-unknown in-domain examples, proof-only predicts \texttt{Unknown} \(1544.0 \pm 30.4\) times and \model{} predicts it \(1525.3 \pm 39.6\) times, so the abstention rate is nearly unchanged. Yet faithful unknown explanations jump from \(0.0\) to \(1444.3 \pm 7.4\). The same effect appears on depth-OOD: predicted unknown counts are \(1278.0 \pm 56.8\) for proof-only and \(1286.0 \pm 72.7\) for \model{}, but faithful unknown explanations jump from \(0.0\) to \(1169.0 \pm 29.0\). The gain therefore comes from turning abstention into a verifiable evidence object, not from simply abstaining more often.

\subsection{Ablating the Abstention Target}

Because the main difference between proof-only supervision and \model{} lies in how unknown cases are supervised, we ablate the abstention target itself. Table~\ref{tab:ablation-results} compares the generic proof-only abstention target to two partial countermodel variants: one with explicit failure tags but no detailed witness, and one with a detailed witness but no explicit failure tag.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\input{generated/ablation_table.tex}
\caption{Ablations on the abstention target. These results test whether the full gain comes from failure tags, witness text, or their combination.}
\label{tab:ablation-results}
\end{table}

The ablation sharpens the mechanism claim. Both partial targets recover most of the gain over proof-only, which localizes the effect to the abstention supervision itself. On the in-domain slice, ProCo-chain and ProCo-witness reach 74.4 and 74.0 joint accuracy, close to full \model{} at 75.0 and far above proof-only at 39.9. On depth-OOD, the same partial variants reach 55.6 and 53.4 joint accuracy versus 26.5 for proof-only. The full target is still best or tied across domains, suggesting that explicit failure tags and detailed missing-support witnesses are complementary rather than redundant.

The scaling table shows two monotonic trends. First, faithfulness and joint accuracy rise steadily with more data. In-domain joint accuracy climbs from \(75.0\) at 4k to \(88.4\) at 16k, \(93.4\) at 32k, and \(96.8\) at full train. On depth-OOD, it climbs from \(55.0\) to \(71.2\), \(77.6\), and \(86.2\). Second, the raw-accuracy penalty shrinks with scale. In-domain, the gap to answer-only narrows from 1.5 points at 4k to 0.3 points at full train. On the fixed depth-5 subset, the gap reverses: \model{} reaches 93.8 accuracy versus 93.1 for answer-only at full train. This is the strongest evidence that countermodel-aware supervision is not merely a low-data calibration trick.

The transfer table shows that the same supervision effect survives outside the original train/test configuration. At 32k train, \model{} reaches 93.7 joint accuracy on NatLang transfer, 92.0 on depth-3 transfer, and 90.7 on birds-electricity. Raw accuracy remains competitive throughout and is actually highest on birds-electricity, where \model{} reaches 91.6 versus 89.8 for answer-only. Combined with the full-train depth-OOD result, this suggests that countermodel-aware supervision is especially helpful when the model must decide whether to commit or abstain under distribution shift.
