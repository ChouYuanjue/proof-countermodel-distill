\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

We evaluate on the processed open-world ProofWriter splits. The main in-domain
setting is depth-3ext-NatLang; the depth-shift setting is depth-5. We compare
three supervision formats: \textbf{answer-only}
predicts only the label, \textbf{proof-only} emits structured reasoning without
explicit missing-support supervision, and \textbf{\model{}} emits proof,
refutation, or abstention witnesses. All reported 7B runs use QLoRA training
for one epoch. The main suite uses Qwen2.5-7B with three sampled 4,096-example
training sets and a fixed 4,000-example evaluation subset.

We report raw accuracy, Unknown F1, evidence faithfulness, and joint accuracy.
Joint accuracy requires both a correct label and verifier-accepted evidence, so
answer-only receives joint score 0 by construction and should be read as a raw
classification baseline.

\subsection{Main Result}

Figure~\ref{fig:protocol} summarizes the fixed-subset protocol, and
Table~\ref{tab:seeded-results} shows the main comparison. At the 4k training
budget, \model{} does not beat answer-only as a raw classifier. Its value is
that the evidence verifies: compared with proof-only, joint accuracy rises from
\(39.9 \pm 0.6\) to \(75.0 \pm 0.7\) in-domain and from \(26.5 \pm 1.1\) to
\(55.0 \pm 0.7\) under depth shift. Raw accuracy remains close to proof-only in
both settings.

\subsection{Why Joint Accuracy Improves}

The gain comes from abstention evidence, not from a higher abstention rate.
Table~\ref{tab:abstention-evidence} restricts evaluation to gold-\texttt{Unknown}
examples. Proof-only predicts \texttt{Unknown} frequently, but produces no
faithful unknown explanations. \model{} converts most of those generic
abstentions into verifier-checkable missing-support witnesses: \(1444.3 \pm
7.4\) in-domain and \(1169.0 \pm 29.0\) under depth shift.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\input{generated/abstention_evidence_table.tex}
\caption{Gold-unknown behavior on the fixed 7B test subset. Faithful means the
model predicts \texttt{Unknown} and emits an accepted missing-support witness;
generic means it predicts \texttt{Unknown} without accepted evidence.}
\label{tab:abstention-evidence}
\end{table}

The tolerant verifier accepts canonical-compatible witnesses, so we also run an
exact-match audit. Table~\ref{tab:abstention-audit} shows that most
unknown-case gains survive exact matching: strict faithfulness is \(71.9 \pm
0.5\) in-domain and \(63.3 \pm 1.5\) under depth shift. The subtype split
matters. Exact \texttt{FAIL[no\_rule]} matching is high, while exact
\texttt{FAIL[ruleX]} matching is much lower. This is expected when multiple
blocked rule applications can explain the same unsupported query, and it marks
where a future verifier should reason about equivalence classes of blocked
derivations instead of one canonical target.

\subsection{Supported-Query Error Profile}

The small raw-accuracy gap to answer-only is not primarily a sign of broad
over-abstention. Table~\ref{tab:error-profile} shows that \model{} stays close
to the proof-only baseline on supported-query accuracy, while the main effect
of the evidence format is a modest rise in support abstention. That rise is
small in-domain and larger under depth shift, where harder queries make the
model more conservative. The same table also shows that most
\texttt{Unknown} predictions are still made on gold-\texttt{Unknown} cases, as
reflected by unknown precision.

\subsection{Support-Deletion Stress Test}

We next test unsupported queries created by intervention. Starting from
provable test questions, we delete one fact or rule from the gold derivation and
keep only mutants where neither the original query nor its opposite is
derivable. These examples are all gold-\texttt{Unknown}, but each has a known
deleted support.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\input{generated/support_deletion_table.tex}
\caption{Support-deletion mutation stress test on 4,000 gold-\texttt{Unknown}

Table~\ref{tab:support-deletion} shows that generic conservatism is not enough.
On Qwen2.5-7B, proof-only predicts \texttt{Unknown} more often than \model{}
but gives zero faithful witnesses; \model{} gives \(2017.7 \pm 40.6\). The
single-seed Mistral-7B check follows the same pattern.

\subsection{Scaling and Transfer}

With more training data, \model{} becomes more attractive: joint accuracy rises
steadily and the raw-accuracy gap to answer-only shrinks. Table~\ref{tab:scaling-results}
summarizes this trend. At full training, \model{} reaches 98.2\% in-domain raw
accuracy with 96.8\% joint accuracy, and 93.8\% depth-shift raw accuracy with
86.2\% joint accuracy.

\subsection{Decoding Cost}

Evidence-bearing outputs are longer than answer-only labels. Table~\ref{tab:decoding-cost}
reports output length and the available observed throughput on support-deletion
runs. In the main ID setting, \model{} outputs average 14.7 whitespace-delimited
words, versus 12.7 for proof-only and 2.0 for answer-only. On support deletion,
answer-only is much faster because it emits only a label, while proof-only and
\model{} have similar throughput; the missing-support witness adds length but
does not materially slow decoding relative to proof-style evidence.
